\begin{abstract}
\noindent
This work was carried out within the \emph{Spot the bot} project of
V.\,A.~Gromov's laboratory and addresses the task of distinguishing texts
written by humans from texts generated by a neural language model, for the
\textbf{Maltese language}~-- a low-resource language of the Semitic family. A
corpus of 36{,}452 human texts was assembled, a word-level LSTM generator was
trained, and for both corpora clouds of $n$-gram vectors based on SVD
embeddings were obtained. The clouds were studied with Wishart clustering and
persistent homology. We show that the cloud of bot texts differs from the human
one in a statistically significant way: it breaks into a larger number of
clusters, has a higher noise fraction, is worse separated (Davies--Bouldin
criterion), and is topologically more complex (more $H_1$ cycles). The
differences are confirmed by a bootstrap estimate ($B=40$, Mann--Whitney test,
$p<10^{-5}$, Cohen's effect size $1.2$--$2.3$).

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} natural language processing, Maltese language,
SVD, latent semantic mapping, LSTM, Wishart clustering, persistent homology,
generated-text detection.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The hypothesis that natural language is a \emph{self-organized critical system}
has been developed in a series of works by V.\,A.~Gromov's
laboratory~\cite{gromov2020,gromov2023}. In this paradigm a text is regarded not
as a linear sequence but as a holistic object possessing geometric and
topological structure in the space of semantic vectors. If the structure of
human language is organized in a particular way, then a text produced by an
artificial language model should differ from a human one precisely at the level
of this structure, even when locally (at the level of individual $n$-grams) it
looks plausible.

The \emph{Spot the bot} project sets the task of testing this hypothesis: to
learn to \textbf{tell a generated text from a human one} by the geometry of the
cloud of its $n$-grams. The project methodology is applied to different
languages; the present work carries out the full cycle for the
\textbf{Maltese language} (\texttt{mt}).

Maltese is an interesting object of study: it is the only Semitic language with
a Latin script and official status in the EU, while at the same time being
low-resource~-- ready-made neural models and large corpora for it are scarce.
This makes the task meaningful from the engineering side as well.

\paragraph{Aim of the work}~-- to run the full \emph{Spot the bot} pipeline for
Maltese and to establish whether the human and generated corpora are
distinguishable by methods of clustering and topological analysis.

\paragraph{Objectives:}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item collect and preprocess a corpus of Maltese texts;
  \item build a dictionary of SVD embeddings;
  \item train a neural generator and obtain the bot corpus;
  \item form clouds of $n$-gram vectors for both corpora;
  \item cluster the clouds with the Wishart algorithm and compare quality
        metrics;
  \item study the clouds by means of persistent homology;
  \item assess the statistical significance of the differences.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Problem statement}

Let a corpus of human texts $\mathcal{H}$ and a corpus of generated texts
$\mathcal{B}$ of comparable size be given. Each word is mapped to a vector of
fixed dimension $m$; $n$ consecutive words give an $n$-gram vector of dimension
$n\cdot m$ (concatenation). The set of all $n$-grams of a corpus forms a
\emph{point cloud} in $\mathbb{R}^{nm}$.

Formally, we test the null hypothesis
\[
  H_0:\quad \text{the clouds }\;\mathcal{H}\;\text{and}\;\mathcal{B}\;
  \text{are statistically indistinguishable by structural characteristics,}
\]
against the alternative $H_1$ of their difference. As structural characteristics
we take: the number and sizes of clusters and the cluster-validity metrics
(internal validity measures, Ch.~23~\cite{aggarwal2013}), as well as
topological invariants~-- Betti numbers and characteristics of persistence
diagrams.

\section{Theoretical background}

\subsection{SVD embeddings and latent semantic mapping}

The main type of embeddings in the project methodology is embeddings based on
the singular value decomposition, going back to Bellegarda's latent semantic
mapping~\cite{bellegarda2005}. From the cleaned corpus a weighted
term--document matrix $A\in\mathbb{R}^{V\times D}$ is built ($V$~-- the number of
unique words, $D$~-- the number of texts) with TF-IDF weights. The truncated
singular value decomposition of rank $k$
\[
  A \;\approx\; U_k\,\Sigma_k\,V_k^{\top},\qquad
  U_k\in\mathbb{R}^{V\times k},\;\;
  \Sigma_k=\operatorname{diag}(\sigma_1,\dots,\sigma_k),
\]
gives a vector representation of a word: the $i$-th row of $U_k\Sigma_k$
corresponds to the $k$-dimensional vector of the $i$-th word. An essential
property of the method: to obtain an embedding of smaller dimension $d<k$ no
repeated decomposition is needed~-- it suffices to take the first $d$ components
of the vector. The decomposition is therefore carried out once with a large $k$
(here $k=1024$).

\subsection{Wishart clustering}

The Wishart algorithm~\cite{wishart1969} is a density-based clustering method
(\emph{mode analysis}) that isolates clusters as regions of high density
separated by regions of low density. The density at a point $x_i$ is estimated
from the distance $r_k(i)$ to its $k$-th nearest neighbour:
\[
  p(x_i) \;=\; \frac{k}{n\cdot V_d\cdot r_k(i)^{\,d}},
  \qquad
  V_d=\frac{\pi^{d/2}}{\Gamma(d/2+1)},
\]
where $n$~-- the number of points, $d$~-- the dimension, $V_d$~-- the volume of
the unit $d$-dimensional ball. Points are processed in order of decreasing
density; for the current point the already-processed clusters it is connected to
are determined via the $k$-nearest-neighbour graph:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item no connections~-- a new cluster is formed;
  \item one cluster~-- the point joins it (if it is not ``completed'');
  \item several clusters~-- the \emph{significant} clusters (with a density gap
        $\ge h$) are frozen and the point goes to noise; the insignificant ones
        are merged.
\end{itemize}
The parameters of the algorithm are $k$ (the number of neighbours for the
density estimate) and $h$ (the significance threshold). The quality of the
resulting clustering is assessed by internal validity measures
(Ch.~23~\cite{aggarwal2013}): silhouette, Davies--Bouldin index,
Calinski--Harabasz index.

\subsection{Persistent homology}

Persistent homology~\cite{edelsbrunner2010,zhu2013} is a tool of topological
data analysis. From a point cloud a filtration of Vietoris--Rips complexes is
built: as the parameter $\varepsilon$ grows, points that are within distance
$\le\varepsilon$ are joined by edges and simplices are formed. During the
filtration topological features ($H_0$~-- connected components, $H_1$~--
one-dimensional cycles) are ``born'' and ``die''. The persistence diagram
records pairs (birth, death); long-lived features are considered significant,
short-lived ones~-- noise. Numerical characteristics: Betti numbers $b_0,b_1$,
total and maximal persistence, persistence entropy
\[
  E=-\sum_i \frac{\ell_i}{L}\,\log\frac{\ell_i}{L},
  \qquad \ell_i\;-\;\text{the lifetime of the}\;i\text{-th feature},\;\;
  L=\sum_i\ell_i.
\]

\subsection{Preprocessing}

Preprocessing reduces word forms to their base form (lemmatization) and replaces
function parts of speech with special tokens: pronouns~-- with \texttt{PRON1},
numerals~-- with \texttt{ORDINAL1}, proper names~-- with \texttt{PERSON1};
punctuation and symbols are discarded. The replacements rely on the part of
speech, so the lemmatizer needs POS tagging.

\paragraph{Three lemmatization variants.} Since Maltese is low-resource and no
reliable ready-made lemmatizer exists for it, three variants were implemented
and compared:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \texttt{L1}~-- the neural lemmatizer \texttt{Stanza} (the
        \texttt{tokenize\,/\,pos\,/\,lemma} pipeline). POS tagging is reliable,
        but neural lemmas for a low-resource language are often inaccurate and
        not backed lexically by anything.
  \item \texttt{L3}~-- a purely rule-based preprocessor without NLP libraries
        (strippers of Maltese affixes: hyphenated articles \texttt{il-},
        \texttt{it-}, \ldots, apostrophe proclitics \texttt{b'},
        \texttt{f'}, \ldots, possessive and object suffixes), close in spirit
        to Subkhangulov's lemmatizer for Tatar. It is ``strict by the
        methodology'' but does not yield parts of speech, so the function-word
        replacements are performed only heuristically (by lists and case)
        rather than by the actual POS.
  \item \texttt{L2}~-- a combined one: the POS tag and the base lemma are taken
        from \texttt{Stanza}, after which our own \emph{lexicon} lemmatizer is
        applied, based on the Maltese morphological database Ġabra
        (1{,}303{,}378 word forms).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{How the L2 lemmatizer works.} For each word form $w$ (if its part of
speech is not a function one and is not discarded) the lemma is determined by a
cascade of lookups in the Ġabra table; the first step that fires gives the
result:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item exact lookup of $w$ (lower-cased) in Ġabra;
  \item lookup of the form with diacritics removed
        (\texttt{ċġħż}$\to$\texttt{cghz});
  \item lookup after stripping the article/proclitic
        (\texttt{il-}, \texttt{b'}, \ldots), including without diacritics;
  \item fallback to the lemma proposed by \texttt{Stanza};
  \item if nothing is found~-- the original word form itself.
\end{enumerate}
Thus L2 combines the strengths of L1 and L3: a reliable POS tag for the
function-word replacements~-- and an accurate, lexically verified lemma instead
of an unreliable neural one. The distribution of the sources of the final lemmas
across the corpus: Ġabra~-- $\approx4.1\,\%$ of tokens (form correction),
the \texttt{Stanza} neural lemma~-- $<0.1\,\%$, the remaining $\approx95.9\,\%$
coincide with the original form (the word is already in its base form or is
absent from Ġabra). The low share of actually changed tokens reflects the
analyticity of the corpus and the coverage limits of the lexicon for a
low-resource language.

\paragraph{Choice of variant.} \texttt{L2} was chosen as the working variant: it
yields the most accurate genuinely Maltese lemmas while preserving the POS
replacements required by the methodology, whereas \texttt{L1} is lexically
weaker and \texttt{L3} loses information about parts of speech. The entire
downstream pipeline (SVD dictionary, generator, $n$-grams, clustering, topology)
is built on the \texttt{L2} corpus. Result: 36{,}452 cleaned texts, about
39.3~million word tokens; the output is a single file, one line~-- one text.

\section{Building the embeddings}

\subsection{SVD dictionary}

From the cleaned corpus, a term--document matrix was built using
\texttt{TfidfVectorizer}. The Maltese alphabet (including the letters
``ċ, ġ, ħ, ż'') together with digits, hyphen and apostrophe was given as
the \texttt{token\_pattern}~-- this cuts off punctuation and foreign-language
insertions. A sparse matrix of size $93{,}343\times36{,}452$ (density $0.0032$)
was obtained.

A truncated singular value decomposition of rank $k=1024$ was applied to the
matrix. The singular values decrease from $\sigma_1\approx39.7$ to
$\sigma_{1024}\approx2.3$. The result is a dictionary
$\{\text{word}\mapsto\text{vector}\in\mathbb{R}^{1024}\}$ for 93{,}343 words.

\subsection{word2vec}

For comparison, word2vec embeddings were also built (CBOW and Skip-gram,
dimension 100, window 5) on the same corpus with \texttt{gensim}
(vocabulary~-- 107{,}405 words).

\section{Generating the bot corpus}

The bot corpus is produced by a neural generator trained on the human corpus. A
\textbf{word-level LSTM} language model was used: it operates on the same
lemma tokens as the SVD dictionary, so the generated texts are directly suitable
for building $n$-grams.

\paragraph{Architecture and training.} Model: an embedding layer (300), a
two-layer LSTM (hidden size 512, dropout $0.3$), a linear layer onto the
vocabulary (the 50{,}000 most frequent words); $44.4$~million parameters in
total. Training~-- next-token prediction with truncated BPTT, the Adam
optimizer, 8 epochs. The perplexity on the held-out set decreased from 164
(epoch~1) to \textbf{98} (epoch~8).

\paragraph{Generation.} From the trained model, 36{,}452 texts were
generated~-- the same number as in the human corpus. The length of each text is
drawn from the empirical distribution of human text lengths, which ensures
comparability of the corpora by volume. The resulting bot corpus: 36{,}452
texts, $29.6$~million tokens.

\section{The $n$-gram dataset}

For each corpus, $n$-grams are extracted with a sliding window of length $n$;
the vector of each word is taken from the SVD dictionary (the first $m$
components), and the word vectors of the $n$-gram are concatenated into a vector
of dimension $n\cdot m$. If at least one word of the $n$-gram is absent from the
dictionary, the $n$-gram is skipped.

The clouds were formed for several configurations $(n,m)$; each cloud contains
300{,}000 $n$-gram vectors for the human and for the bot. The fraction of
$n$-grams skipped because of a missing word grows with $n$ (for $n=3$~-- about
20\,\%, for $n=5$~-- over 95\,\%), which is natural: the probability that
\emph{all} words of a long $n$-gram are in the dictionary falls as $n$ grows.

\section{Wishart clustering: experiments}

\subsection{Parameter sweep}

First, the sensitivity of the algorithm to the parameters $k$ and $h$ was
studied on the configuration $n=4,\;m=8$. The significance parameter $h$ turned
out to be practically inert: when varied from $0.2$ to $125$ the results did not
change~-- in a high-dimensional cloud the density gaps within clusters greatly
exceed any reasonable $h$. The parameter $k$, on the contrary, strongly affects
the granularity of the partition: $k=7$ gives about 400 clusters, $k=51$~-- about
30. The noise fraction is high for any parameters (75--87\,\%)~-- this is a
structural property of density-based clustering in a high-dimensional space. For
the further experiments $k=11,\;h=1$ were fixed.

\subsection{Comparison of configurations}

Table~\ref{tab:configs}. In \emph{all} configurations the bot cloud breaks into
a \emph{larger} number of clusters than the human one

\subsection{Assessing statistical significance}

To make sure the differences are not an artifact of a particular subsample, a
bootstrap estimate was carried out: $B=40$ times a new independent subsample of
30{,}000 $n$-grams was drawn for the human and for the bot, and for each one
the Wishart clustering was computed. Since the human and bot subsamples are
independent, the \emph{two-sample} Mann--Whitney test was applied; the confidence
interval was built for the \emph{mean} difference $\Delta=\overline{x}_B-
\overline{x}_H$ via the standard error, and the effect size~-- Cohen's $d$.

\section{Discussion}

Two independent methods~-- density-based clustering and topological analysis~--
give a consistent result. The human $n$-gram cloud is \textbf{more
consolidated}: fewer clusters, a pronounced dense core (a larger largest
cluster), fewer topological cycles. The bot cloud is \textbf{more fragmented and
topologically more complex}: more clusters, a higher noise fraction, worse
cluster separation, more $H_1$ cycles.

In substantive terms this can be interpreted as follows: a word-level LSTM,
learning to predict the next word, reproduces \emph{local} collocability well,
but does not reproduce the \emph{global} organization of the semantic space~--
a human text ``contracts'' toward a stable core of themes and constructions,
whereas a generated one spreads out into many small, poorly separated clumps.

\paragraph{Limitations.} The noise fraction in the clustering is high (about
72\,\%)~-- a consequence of the curse of dimensionality; the absolute values of
the metrics should be treated with caution, whereas the \emph{differences}
between the corpora are stable and significant. The significance parameter $h$
of the Wishart algorithm turned out to be uninformative in a high-dimensional
space. The word2vec embeddings, under a naive truncation of components, are
unusable~-- a separate adaptation is required.

\paragraph{Directions for development.} It makes sense to: study the influence
of the generation temperature and of the LSTM quality on distinguishability;
compare with other generator architectures; apply feature normalization before
clustering; extend the topological analysis to $H_2$ and to comparing diagrams
by the Wasserstein metric.

\section{Conclusion}

In this work the full pipeline of the \emph{Spot the bot} project was carried
out for Maltese: a corpus of 36{,}452 texts was collected and preprocessed, an
SVD dictionary was built ($93{,}343\times1024$), a word-level LSTM generator was
trained (perplexity 98) and a bot corpus of comparable size was produced,
$n$-gram clouds were formed, and Wishart clustering and topological analysis
were carried out.

\textbf{The main result:} the human and generated corpora are
\emph{statistically significantly distinguishable} by the structure of the
$n$-gram cloud. Four of the five clustering metrics distinguish them with
$p<10^{-5}$ and a large effect size; the topological analysis independently
confirms this: the bot cloud contains more one-dimensional cycles. Thus, for the
Maltese language, the project's original hypothesis is confirmed: a generated
text differs from a human one at the level of the holistic geometric and
topological structure.
